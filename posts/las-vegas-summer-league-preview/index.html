Players to Watch at the Las Vegas Summer League

Author

Dillon Siders

Published

July 12, 2024

The long wait is finally over. Every NBA team will be back in action for the 2024 Las Vegas Summer league. Even though a few teams have gotten a look at their incoming rookie classes the majority of the league will get to see their debuts starting Friday. Not only will we see the debut of the 2024 rookie class, but the first look at some second year players and their developments after an entire NBA season. Without further adieu let’s jump into some of my must watch players for 2024 Summer League.

Brandon Miller

When the Hornets Summer League roster was announced I was shocked to see Brandon Miller on it. In recent years it has become more common for players who had good rookie seasons to sit out of Summer League in year two. Miller decided to take the opposite approach even after finishing 3rd in Rookie of the Year race and earning All-Rookie first team honors. Much like Miller’s idol, Paul George, he showcased a versatile offensive skill set centered around his handle and pull up shooting at 6 ’9. NBA teams are always looking for bigger wings that can create for themselves like Jayson Tatum and Kevin Durant, and Charlotte seems to have found one in Miller.

The main thing I want to see from Miller this Summer League is improvements getting others involved. Miller wasn’t bad as a playmaker finishing with a 1.32 assist to turnover ratio, but his assist%(11.6) and turnover%(10.2) show that there is room for him to grow. Even though his primary role will be scoring the ball even the best scorers have to be able to find teammates when defenses focus on stopping them. Jayson Tatum, Paul George, and Kevin Durant had similar numbers early in their careers before making leaps to over 20% in the assist% category. If Miller can follow the same path as those players he could be a leading factor behind the resurgence of the Charlotte Hornets.

Anthony Black

Coming out of Arkansas, Anthony Black was one of my favorite players in the draft. Positional size and switchable defenders have become a coveted entity by NBA teams and Black provides just that. Although he did not play a massive role, only 16.9 minutes per game, he flashed major potential as a point of attack defender using great technique to slide with ball handlers and keep them in front. He also brings a lot of disruption with steals and deflections thanks to length and anticipation skills. As Black continues to mature he could become one of the more versatile defenders in the NBA.

Summer League should provide Black the perfect opportunity to showcase if he grew on the offensive end. Defense has always been his calling card, but you cannot expect to get consistent minutes without some impact on the offensive end. There were some areas that Black showed promise including his rim finishing(68.4%) and improved 3-point jumper(39.4%), but both of those were on limited attempts and mostly assisted. Summer League will put the ball back in his hands and let us see if Black can maintain those numbers on self created looks. If Black developed on the offensive end he could bring another dimension to an already versatile Orlando core.

Cam Whitmore

After slipping out of the lottery on draft night in 2023, the Rockets scooped up the high flying wing out of Villanova. Nobody can question the untapped potential Whitmore has with his athleticism and shot creation skills at such a young age. He displayed both of those skills early with the Rockets being a microwave off the bench that could easily put points on the board. With a little more refinement Whitmore’s athleticism and offensive skill could be the perfect long-term complement to Alperen Sengun’s playmaking and post game.

During Summer League I want to see Whitmore how Whitmore makes an impact besides scoring the basketball. Most of the questions around Whitmore are about his lack of feel in terms of playmaking and the technical side of defense. His athleticism allows him to easily create advantages as a driver, but his lack of vision/playmaking skill makes you wonder if he can fully utilize those advantages. Similarly on defense his athleticism and length are the perfect tools to become a disruptive defender, but he still lacks some of the understanding more polished NBA defenders have. After a full year with NBA coaching Whitmore should take some steps in the positive direction, but I do not expect major strides since he is still so young.

Kobe Bufkin

Out of all the players mentioned, Bufkin saw the least amount of NBA action only appearing in only 17 NBA games as a rookie. While he made a limited NBA impact Bufkin showed his potential playing for Atlanta’s G League affiliate. In 14 G League games Bufkin put up impressive numbers of 23.6 points, 5.4 rebounds, and 5.9 assists per game. His skills as a combo guard that can play on or off the ball will be very important next to a star guard like Trae Young who has the ball in their hands most of the time.

This summer I do not have any specific skills I want to see Bufkin improve at, instead I just want to see more minutes from him. While I love the sport I do not plan my schedule around watching the College Park Skyhawks. Being a massive Bufkin fan during his draft cycle is my main reasoning behind my interest in watching him during Summer League. One of the skills that Bufkin will need if he wants a role on this Hawks team is consistent perimeter shooting. In the G he shot 35%, but in limited NBA action that number dipped to 25%, albeit on 40 total attempts. While Summer League is not an indicator of whether a player will have success in the NBA, I cannot wait to see Bufkin back in action after a whole NBA season’s worth of development.
