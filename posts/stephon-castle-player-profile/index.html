Point Guard or Two-Way Wing? 2024 NBA Prospect Profile: Stephon Castle

Author

Dillon Siders

Published

June 14, 2024

Going into this college basketball season UConn was looking to become the eighth team to consecutive national championships. Behind the returning core of Donovan Clingan, Tristan Newton, and Alex Kararban UConn just needed to fill out its roster around them. Dan Hurley went to work getting sharpshooter Cam Spencer to transfer, but he also convinced one of the best players in the 2023 recruiting class to take a backseat to focus on competing for a championship

This recruit was Stephon Castle. In high school, Castle was used to having the ball in his hands as a true point guard. At UConn, this was the opposite. Due to Hurley’s system revolving around player movement and quick decisions, there really isn’t a need for a true point guard. Castle adapted to this change phenomenally becoming one of the most impactful players in college this year.

Now with the season over and Castle focused on his future there has been lots of discourse on what his future NBA role is. One sect consisting of Castle believers feels he can blossom into a point guard with time, while the evaluators less in on him believe the wing role is best. Before I get into what I think his role will be let’s take a deeper look at Castle’s game as a whole.

Scoring/Shooting

Castle’s scoring is a major topic of debate. As a scorer, his game is fairly simplistic at the moment. His greatest strength scoring wise is his ability to finish at the rim. Even though Castle was a freshman he did not look the part physically. Castle was great at using his strength to get to the front of the rim before using his soft touch to finish. I also like his footwork and patience as a driver. When he got into a crowded paint Castle used pivots, spins, and euros to finish around defenders.

Other areas I see Castle making a major impact on offense are in transition and off-ball as a cutter. Both of these allow Castle to show his natural athleticism and IQ. He can be a grab-and-go threat thanks to his guard skills, or he can run the wings and wait for someone to find him up the floor. Off the ball, Castle can still be a threat by making smart relocations and cuts to get himself easy looks.

The main reason why some evaluators have questions about Castle’s scoring at the next level is his shooting. At UConn he was very inconsistent from 3 and in the mid-range. Having a respectable jump shot will be key for Castle’s role whether he’s a point guard or wing. While I doubt he ever becomes a high-volume or high-efficiency 3-point shooter I think he does enough in other areas to make up for it.

Passing/Playmaking

Castle’s passing is one of the main reasons why people believe he can be a traditional point guard. For UConn, he had 99 assists to 51 turnovers giving him a nearly 2:1 assist-to-turnover ratio. While this does not mean everything it does match up with the film. I personally believe that Castle is one of the higher-IQ players in this class and his playmaking is a major reason for that.

The thing about Castle’s passing that stood out most to me was how quickly he made decisions. It seemed like he already knew where he was going to pass before he even caught it. His quick decision-making perfectly complements his downhill driving and cutting. When Castle can draw a help defender he easily finds the open man whether it be a spot-up shooter or a big man under the basket. This makes Castle the perfect connective passer to keep offenses flowing.

I also think Castle’s court vision is very impressive. Most of the traits he showed as a scorer like IQ and patience transfer to his passing. This is best shown in transition where Castle is always willing and ready to advance the ball. If Castle can’t find a teammate running the floor he will look to push the pace himself trying to put pressure on the defense. He can then leverage this pressure to find the open man.

My intrigue with Castle as a playmaker comes with some of the advanced flashes he’s shown. He had some fun pick-and-roll passing reps with Donovan Clingan, including their go-to empty-side pick-and-roll with the screen set in the mid-range. He also showcased some fun one-handed and live dribble passes that could raise his ceiling as a passer if done more consistently.

Defense

From day one in the NBA Stephon Castle will be a good defender. He was UConn’s primary on-ball defender this year which many did not expect coming in. Like I mentioned before Castle was more of an offensive-focused guard in high school so many did not expect the level of defensive play he showcased. Playing next to so many talented players allowed Castle to put a greater focus on the defensive side of the ball this year.

All year long team’s top ball handlers who faced UConn were hounded and harassed by Castle. The combination of strength, length, and lateral movement makes it tough for offensive players to go around, through, or over him. This natural athleticism is paired with great technique. Castle consistently gets low in his stance and slides his feet with defenders. The final piece of a potentially elite on-ball defender is screen navigation which Castle certainly has. All year he consistently slithered around on ball screens and kept ball handlers from getting past him.

Castle did not force a ton of steals, but I think that is the byproduct of the type of defender he is. Castle barely took any risks instead focusing on applying suffocating pressure and keeping players in front of him. Personally, I’d rather have a Castle type defender than one that is less consistent but generates more turnovers. While he doesn’t force turnovers by jumping passing lanes Castle can use his strength to stonewall drivers and make them lose the ball.

Is Castle a Point Guard?

Deciding whether Castle is a traditional point guard or not is mostly based on your definition of what a point guard is. For me, a traditional point guard is someone who handles a bulk of the ball-handling reps and has the responsibility to keep the offense running smoothly while setting up teammates. While Castle doesn’t meet my definition of a traditional point guard we have seen that archetype becoming less common.

Let’s look at the NBA finals this year. Do the Mavericks or Celtics have a traditional point guard? While Luka Doncic could qualify he still shares on-ball reps and playmaking responsibilities with Kyrie Irving. What about Boston? Jrue Holiday and Derrick White wouldn’t meet the requirements I stated above either. As the NBA continues to evolve I think we will see a continued focus on sharing ball handling and playmaking duties between multiple players.

With all the skills Castle has shown I would be disappointed if he didn’t at least get a chance to play with the ball in his hands. The level of IQ, dribbling, and playmaking he has are all good enough to be a point guard even if it’s as a complementary piece. Also if you take him to play point and it ends up that he can’t be a point guard you can just make him a two-way connective wing. Having the fallback path of being a wing gives me a major boost of confidence in selecting Castle with an early pick.
