2024 NBA Draft Prospect Profile: Tidjane Salaun

Author

Dillon Siders

Published

May 28, 2024

The 2024 NBA Draft is one of mystery and uncertainty. Throughout this draft cycle, evaluators have gone back and forth on almost every single player. While one outlet has a player ranked in the top 5 another might not even have them in the lottery. One of these players who seemed to have a gigantic draft range is now solidifying himself as THE upside swing/project player of the 2024 draft for many NBA teams. This player is French wing Tidjane Salaun.

Salaun is a 6’9-6’10 wing who has tantalizing upside. His mixture of youth and physical tools has teams buzzing and the rumor mill swirling. Recently it has been said that NBA teams are a lot higher on Salaun than the media, usually implying late lottery as his floor on draft night. This was surprising to me.

In my previous evaluations, I viewed Salaun as more of a high upside swing in the mid to late 20s than a lottery pick. While he showed some great flashes there was a lot to be desired with Salaun. These recent rumors made me want to rewatch his film and see if I was missing something. After rewatching Salaun I was left with a lot of questions and a lot of intrigue.

Since this is my first player profile I want to give a little preface on what to expect. I’ll start by going over what I believe to be a prospect’s strengths or the reasons to draft them. After their strengths, I’ll cover their areas of improvement. Finally, I’ll give my high-end NBA role and low-end NBA role for the prospect. I think this helps provide some context for all the possible outcomes for a player. Now, let’s get into one of the most polarizing names of the 2024 NBA draft.

The first thing that stood out when watching Salaun was his physical stature. At the spry age of 18 Salaun already looks like one of the biggest players on the court in a professional league. We always see players get an inch or two added to their listed height, but if I had to guess Salaun is closer to 6’10 than 6’8. It’s not only Salaun’s height, but all his athletic tools jump out to me. His mix of height, length, strength, and vertical pop all make him athletically ready for the NBA.

Since Salaun is, in my opinion, a very raw prospect, there are not a lot of skills that can be pointed to as strengths currently. A lot of Salaun’s evaluation is projection and that is scary for a prospect. One of the things that makes me believe in Salaun’s ability to improve is his work ethic. Outside of his size, his motor is what stood out most. Salaun ran the floor in transition, was active on the offensive glass, and made some plays in passing lanes/as a rotation defender. While he still lacks consistency in those areas Salaun’s willingness to play hard gives me hope in his long-term development.

Now we start to get into some of the more concerning things about Salaun. I do want to mention that Salaun is one of those “late-bloomers” in terms of draft prospects. Salaun only started playing basketball in recent years compared to some who have played their entire lives. This inexperience is one of the main causes of Salaun’s deficiencies.

The area of most importance for Salaun’s NBA future is his jump shot. Most evaluators coin Salaun as a 3-and-D wing meaning that his 3-point shooting is a necessity. Currently, Salaun’s outlook as a shooter is pretty murky. This year he has shot just below 33% on 140 total attempts. While those numbers are pretty underwhelming there are reasons for this. First Salaun just is not consistent yet. I believe in his mechanics, has a high release point, and usually sets his feet well. Second, he doesn’t make shooting easy on himself. Salaun showed that he had tons of confidence in his jumper having a quick trigger in halfcourt and transition. When Salaun was open it did not matter if he was towing the line or a couple of steps behind it, the shot was going up. There were also a few times when Salaun was spotting up where he caught a kick out but did not know what he wanted to do before catching the ball. This resulted in bad misses, sloppy drives, and turnovers.

All of these factors make projecting Salaun as a shooter very difficult. I lean to think that he will eventually become a league-average spot-up shooter since I believe in his mechanics. I don’t think I can project him as a movement shooter or pull-up shooter because his mechanics were very inconsistent on the move and poor ballhandling.

Continuing with Salaun’s scoring he has yet to show any type of midrange game and minimal stuff as a finisher. Once again I’ll bring up Salaun’s touch or lack thereof. While Salaun has some touch on his jumper and as a finisher, I would call them flashes more than skills. This paired with a shaky handle and low feel makes it hard for me to see him improving at creating pull-up opportunities or consistently getting to the rim. Luckily for Salaun his size, length, and athleticism can make up for some of the issues.

The final part of Salaun’s offensive game to analyze is his passing and playmaking. I’ll keep this short, it’s pretty bad. This area of Salaun’s game is the one most impacted by this inexperience. On the year Salaun had 34 assists to 50 turnovers, not great. On film, it looked to be more about lack of feel than being a bad/unwilling passer. With Salaun it just looked like the game moved a tick too fast at times. Either he would take too long to make decisions or pre-determine them. Usually, I believe that feel is something that cannot be taught, but Salaun is slightly different because of his inexperience. As he accumulates more minutes on the court and learns under NBA coaches he could develop a better understanding of the nuances of basketball.

On defense, Salaun is once again a work in progress. Like most other parts of his game, the physical tools and motor give him the perfect starting point to become a great defender. Being 6’9 with long arms should allow him to be disruptive off-ball in passing lanes or contesting help side. A mixture of vertical pop and strength also gives Salaun the potential to eventually guard bigs.

In its current state Salaun’s defense would likely keep him off an NBA court in any game that matters. Similar to his playmaking, Salaun’s defense is affected by his lack of natural basketball feel. I don’t know if there was a single-shot fake that he did not jump at this season. That paired with messy closeouts and bad footwork led to a good amount of blowbyes. As Salaun gets older hopefully he stops jumping at so many pump fakes and cleans up the other aspects he could become a solid defender.

After reevaluating Tidjane Salaun’s film I was left with a lot of the same takeaways, but also some newfound intrigue. He still has a long way to go as a player before making an impact, but there are not many upside swings I would take in Salaun’s range over him. While he only moved from a late first-early second on my board to the 22-25 range I can see why a team would take him sooner.

In this age of the NBA big, athletic wings that shoot and play defense are some of the most valuable players in the league. Salaun is one of the best bets to become that in this mysterious draft class so do not be surprised if an NBA team gambles on his upside come draft night. If Salaun can capitalize on his physical tools I could see him playing a role similar to Michael Porter Jr of the Denver Nuggets. If he can’t develop I could see him having a fairly short NBA career.
